Lexical behavior



Lexical behavior involves using specific words, terms, or verbs in a message to enhance the sender’s intention to mislead a receiver. This type consists of four specific behaviors.

1.
The first lexical behavior is using the first-person pronoun.

For example, you can think of a sentence like, “I’m pretty sure that this information can help you a lot.” In this sentence, “I” is a first-person pronoun for the lexical behavior.

2.
The second lexical behavior involves using interaction requesting words that request users to take an action with an online account or a link such as ‘update, confirm, verify, secure, log, click, inconvenient, and account.’

For instance, in the sentence “Click the button below to update your personal information”, the word "click" is used.

3.
The third lexical behavior involves using generalizing terms like ‘everyone, all, anybody.’

For example, in the sentence, “Everyone believes that our economy will improve next year”, a generalizing term ‘everyone’ is used.

4.
The fourth lexical behavior involves using exclusive words such as ‘but, except, without.’

For example, in the sentence, “Benefit is clear without loss”, an exclusive word ‘without’ is used.


Structural behavior



The structural behavior concerns manipulating the structural elements of the emails, such as email address and sent time. This behavior consists of five specific behaviors.

1.
The first behavior involves link-embedding words.

You can think of the words with a link including ‘click here’, ‘log in’, or ‘update’. When email receivers click the link-embedding words, they can go to another web page.

2.
The second behavior is using a specious link.

You can insert a link that contains a fake URL address as the following example. “Refer to the NYT article to read their corruption (www.nytime.net/article/12543).”

3.
The third structural behavior involves using a fake group email address.

For example, All_student_announcement@uncc.edu is a fake group email address.

4.
The fourth structural behavior involves the sent time manipulation.

For instance, you may notice the sent time as an unusual one. A business email sent at 03:35 AM is very uncommon.

5.
The last structural behavior is using email address falsification to make a spurious impression to the receivers.

For example, by using a fake email address, such as insurance@car.com, or bestheadhunter@linkedin.net, email senders can allude to that he or she holds domain expertise, which attempts to hoax email receivers.

Discourse behavior



The discourse can be defined as a sequence of sentences that have internal relations to themselves as well as external to other sentences. The discourse behavior concerns linking part of a message in online interaction to the entire message as a whole that looks beyond individual sentences. There are two specific behaviors.

1.
The first discourse behavior is using mismatch subjects. That is, the subject line of an email is irrelevant or does not match the content in the body of the email.

For example, the subject line of an email is “Time Sensitive Refund Notification from Amazon”, BUT the content of the email body is about a credit card promotion.

2.
The second discourse behavior is using disguised sentences. With this behavior, an email sender disguises the subject line and the content of an email as a reply to the email receiver.

For instance, the body of an email starts with the sentence, “This email is in response to the message that you sent yesterday.”
